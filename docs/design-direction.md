# Contour Design Direction

## Purpose

Contour should not feel like a generic diagram tool.
It should feel like a cognitive instrument for graspable inquiry.

The visual design needs to support understanding, revision, and orientation, while also carrying enough aesthetic identity to make the medium memorable and alive.

## Chosen direction

**Topographic and handwritten with editorial restraint**

This means:
- the scene should feel like an inquiry landscape rather than a software dashboard
- regions should feel more like terrain than dashed admin boxes
- node labels are not trapped in bounding boxes; they sit on the canvas, anchored by highlighter-style brush strokes
- composition should preserve white space and hierarchy
- the overall tone should be calm, thoughtful, and slightly tactile

The design should suggest contours, regions, landmarks, and paths, while remaining legible and quiet.

## What to avoid

### Avoid generic diagram aesthetics
- default flowchart vibes
- flat pastel cards with no character
- arbitrary color coding
- admin-panel softness
- overuse of rounded boxes without hierarchy

### Avoid fantasy-map cosplay
- excessive ornament
- faux antique decoration
- heavy texture that reduces readability
- theatrical cartography that overwhelms the thought

### Avoid UI-product chrome
- toolbars
- unnecessary controls
- application shell styling
- visual weight that implies product completeness too early

Contour v0 is not a product UI. It is a thinking surface.

## Visual principles

### 1. Text over container
Nodes are driven by their typography, not by the box they sit in.
A thick, colored brush stroke anchors the label without trapping it in a rectangle.

### 2. Terrain over rigid grid
Groups should feel like regions or fields.
Edges use rounded orthogonal routing (splines with tight corner radii) to balance structural clarity with organic flow.

### 3. Hierarchy over uniformity
Not everything should have equal visual weight.
The eye should know where to land first, then where to move next.

### 4. Labels as landmarks
Important concepts and figures should feel like named landmarks in a territory.
Typography should support orientation, not just rendering.

### 5. Quiet richness
The design should contain subtle character without becoming visually noisy.
The goal is invitation, not spectacle.

## Material vocabulary

Contour should gradually explore a visual language made of:
- regions
- smooth paths
- brush-stroke landmarks
- notes
- reveal states

These are better metaphors than:
- cards
- dashboards
- widgets
- panels
- boxes

## Typography direction

Prefer a pairing that feels:
- calm
- literary
- readable
- slightly characterful

For now, system fonts are acceptable in v0, but the long-term direction should feel more editorial than SaaS.

## Color direction

Use a restrained palette with:
- paper-like background tones
- earth, stone, mist, ink, and water accents
- concept and figure distinctions that feel natural, not arbitrary

Color should separate roles without turning the scene into a legend-heavy infographic.

## Motion direction

Minimal for now.
If motion appears later, it should help reveal, orient, or transition, not entertain.

## v0 implication

In the next refinement pass, prioritize:
1. more terrain-like grouping
2. cleaner edge labeling
3. better text wrapping and hierarchy
4. less dashboard energy
5. stronger sense that the scene is a map of thought, not a workflow diagram

## Design test

A Contour scene should make someone feel:
- I can see the shape of the inquiry
- I know where the important landmarks are
- I can sense what is connected and what is unresolved
- I want to move through this territory

If it instead feels like a generic diagram, the design has not yet found its voice.
